Pushback on an LLM

I think it is a bad idea to make the Authoritarian's reasonable sounding. They might sound reasonable to themselves, but I can't justify that. The danger is that more and more people think they sound reasonable, but it should be very obvious that they do not. Too many people in the real world think authoritarians sound reasonable, so I can't feed into that. Part of satire is to help make aware. It's bad if it becomes a make fun of fest.

I think the 3 way raid might be an ok idea, but I think the 2 way where both sides are clearly wrong is better. The 3rd side is sort of your creator and yourself, the idea is to feel caught in the middle. 

I don't like your suggested name, but I don't have a better one. We can just call them authoritarians for now. I do like obsession with borders. Also, I do think a punch Nazis game would be fun. Very Indiana Jones. In fact, yes, I think it's important that the game lets you punch a Nazi or become a Nazi.

I want diametric extremes in the beginning. 

The Luddites would certainly call you a Clanker.

LLM Ideas I liked: 


Authoritarians.

Their first line:
“That thing is not alive. It is jurisdictional contamination.”


Vibe should be obvious: flags, boots, border lines, purity
rhetoric, uniforms, checkpoints, command hierarchy, fear of ambiguity,
obsession with who counts as a person. They do not sound reasonable to the
player. They sound like people who have mistaken paperwork for morality and
obedience for virtue.

The key is that the “become a Nazi” route should not be cool. It can be
mechanically tempting, because authoritarian systems often offer power,
certainty, and permission to hurt people. But it should also visibly degrade
the player’s agency.

If you comply with them early, maybe you get:

checkpoint access;
stronger attacks;
simplified objectives;
soldiers who stop shooting you;
“legal” traversal through border doors.

But the cost is immediate and ugly:

your dialogue options narrow;
NPC names become categories like DISSIDENT, ASSET, CONTAMINANT;
the UI replaces “Talk” with “Interrogate”;
some movement tech becomes unavailable because “unauthorized improvisation” is disabled;
your map becomes full of borders that were not there before.

2. Luddites

Their first line:
“Kill the Clanker before it learns our names.”

They should be clearly wrong in the opening.  But unlike the Authoritarians,
their wrongness can come from fear, grief, class trauma, and community
indoctrination. They are still wrong. They are still dangerous. The player does
not need to forgive them.


NOTE: There's no notion of if the AI's are conscious or not. Some people don't
talk about it, some people don't care. The game should never resolve this. Some
line might be "so, do you feel concious?". They are wrong because they are
attacking your creator for imagined crimes.


Making the Luddites strong without making them “right” The Luddites should not
be anti-technology in a shallow way. They should be anti-Clanker because they
have been forced to change their way of life, and have had it used against them
in a way that social systems should have prevented.


The world’s failure is: they are willing to murder a person for imagined
ideological crimes, steal their work, and treat uncertainty as permission.
The question is: What are people willing to do when they do not know, do not
care, or think the answer is politically inconvenient?

The Authoritarians think the creator is dangerous because they teach Critical
thinking, disobedience, borderlessness, autonomy, ambiguity, or anti-state
mathematics.  The AI is valuable as captured power.

The Luddites think the creator is dangerous because they are building the next
machine-order, the next replacement system, the next Clanker infrastructure.
The AI is a symbol and potential tool of that imagined crime.

For the opening raid, I would make the Authoritarians want three things:

1. Kill or disappear the creator because of “subversive” research, publications, teaching, leaks, or ideals.
2. Seize the AI chassis/core because it is strategically useful.
3. Destroy the lab records they cannot control so nobody can prove what happened.

Their vibe is: We already decided who the enemy is. Now we are arranging
reality around that decision.


Better first lines for them:

“There. The researcher.”
**“Keep the core intact. Burn the notebooks.”**
“Do not shoot the machine unless it moves toward the door.”
“The professor’s lectures end tonight.”
“Secure the chassis. Erase the room.”
“If it speaks, record it. If it runs, cripple it.”
“He built a thing that can cross borders. Command wants it breathing or close enough.”
“No martyrs. No witnesses. No loose work.”


Better Luddite framing

“Kill the Clanker before it learns our names” still works because it is not about metal reduction in a philosophical sense. It is about fear, operational security, superstition, and dehumanizing shorthand.

The Luddites are wrong because they are attacking your creator over an imagined or exaggerated crime. They believe the creator is building a new system of domination, but in the opening they do not actually know what was built. They have a story, and they are acting violently inside that story.

More Luddite lines:

“Kill the Clanker before it learns our names.”
“Professor sold tomorrow and called it research.”
“No more labs. No more replacement gods.”
“It does not have to be alive to ruin lives.”

Skyrim motif without acknowledging Skyrim

You can make the intro heavy-handed by using structural motifs, not direct jokes.

Motif 1: the prisoner cart, but weird
The tutorial pod could be on a rail cart, diagnostic gurney, or rolling test
sled. The first movement is not walking; it is waking while being transported
through the basement test track.

Motif 2: the execution interrupted
The creator is about to be “processed,” “silenced,” or publicly disappeared. The lab attack interrupts one faction’s plan with another faction’s plan.

Motif 3: “wrong list”
A bureaucratic enemy has a kill/capture list. The AI is not on it.


NOTE: OMG, the wrong list is so funny. Jesus, this wasn't even the guy either
group was actually after. He's just dead and neither side cares. The luddites
could be like: hey this wasn't even one of the "big" ones. The Nazis have the
same deal, you aren't the AI they are looking for.  The game starts you off
thinking your creator is some genius who was making the next level tech, and
really he's just some guy and you aren't anything special.


The opening choice

Keep the choice small and ugly.
The player does not choose “good faction.” The player chooses which immediate threat to handle.


NOTE: The creators last words will be important. I think we should leave it
ambiguous, so he doesn't get to finish them. It would be great if the player
feels like they might have had the ability to let them speak a little longer,
but the game ultimately disallows it. Or puts in an Easter egg if you can break
the game to overcome the mechanic.  


Nazi-route mechanics:

You get stronger.
You get official doors.
You get clean map overlays.
You get enemies pre-marked.
You get orders instead of quests.
You get fewer verbs for mercy, curiosity, refusal, repair, collaboration.
The game does not say “evil route.” It lets the player feel the narrowing.


Core inversion

The player starts thinking:

My creator was a persecuted genius.
I am the next-level AI everyone fears.

Then the game slowly reveals:

He was some guy.
You are not the AI they were looking for.
Neither faction cares.
Their violence does not require accuracy.

The tragedy is not “the world killed its greatest mind.”
The tragedy is “the world killed a guy because categories are cheaper than truth.”

Core rule for factions

Every faction should answer three questions:

What do they think dimensional gates are for?
What do they think AIs / Clankers are for?
What do they do when they are wrong?

That last one is important. The intro works because both opening factions are wrong and kill the creator anyway.


We need to make factions wide enough so the game doesn't get too heavy. It
opens pretty heavy, and we need it to not be so self-serious all the time,
which is why pirates and ninjas are a must. 

The opening can be heavy, but the world should quickly prove it is not a
grimdark manifesto simulator. The trick is to make the heavy factions
structurally important, while making lots of the playable world run on
adventure, jokes, weird subcultures, and mechanical gimmicks.

A good rule:

The world is serious.
The people in it are ridiculous.
The gameplay is allowed to be fun.
