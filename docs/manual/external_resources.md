## Start with these sources

* **PC Gamer — “How to design a great Metroidvania map”**: the most useful practical interview I found. Team Cherry discusses blocking out rooms with 64×64 black tiles, testing jump spacing before dressing rooms, building a coherent world, adding many inter-area connections, and making “getting lost” still produce discoveries. ([PC Gamer][1])
* **Game Maker’s Toolkit — “Boss Keys” playlist**: probably the best video-analysis series on nonlinear world design, with episodes on Metroid, Super Metroid, Symphony of the Night, Metroid Prime, Hollow Knight, Metroid Dread, and Silksong. ([YouTube][2])
* **Hugo Bille — “Analysis of Super Metroid”**: deep moment-by-moment level-design study of Super Metroid, especially how it teaches invisibly, manages exploration scope, uses map rooms and visual hints, and later rewards open exploration. ([Internet Archive][3])
* **Game Developer — “The undying allure of the Metroidvania”**: broad interviews with Igarashi, Tom Happ, Guacamelee devs, and others. Best for philosophy: discovery, player agency, soft locks, replayability, and why the world itself is the main object of design. ([Game Developer][4])
* **Game Developer — “The Foundation of Metroidvania Design”**: a concise checklist-style piece: movement must feel good from minute one, upgrades should change how the player plays rather than function as simple keys, and environments must evolve around the player’s growing moveset. ([Game Developer][5])
* **Dreamnoid — “How to create your own Metroidvania”**: one of the best solo/small-team practical writeups. It covers biome planning, ability order, ROI, reusable abilities, enemy theming, collectibles, environmental storytelling, and room-stamping workflows. ([Dreamnoid][6])
* **Moon Studios / Ori interview — “Designing Ori and the Will of the Wisps”**: excellent on blockout workflow, polish, physics-based traversal, and why a Metroidvania world becomes “one huge puzzle” where changes in one area can break another. ([Game Developer][7])
* **Koji Igarashi interviews on Game Developer**: useful for fundamentals: feedback, fun-first design, responsive controls, fan expectations, jumps, hitboxes, and the differences between 2D-feeling gameplay and 3D assets in 2D spaces. ([Game Developer][8])
* **Bandai Namco / Denfaminicogamer Metroidvania roundtable**: surprisingly useful on difficulty, RPG growth, save-point placement, player “wandering speed,” map tempo, and the risk/reward of deciding whether to push forward or turn back. ([Bandai Namco Entertainment][9])
* **Dead Cells level-design writeup**: best source I found for procedural or hybrid design. Sébastien Bénard says pure procedural generation hurt logical consistency and immersion, so Dead Cells used a restrained hybrid approach with handmade structure and pacing beats. ([Game Developer][10])
* **Kayin — “The Mechanics of a Metroidvania are Tools, not the Destination”**: sharp contrarian essay. The core advice is not to copy genre furniture—colored locks, bomb walls, map icons, fast travel—without asking what emotional experience those mechanics serve. ([Kayinworks][11])
* **The Level Design Book — “Wayfinding”**: not Metroidvania-specific, but very relevant. It argues for wayfinding as player agency and information design, rather than “tricking” the player down a critical path. ([Level Design Book][12])

## The advice that kept recurring

* **Make movement fun immediately.** A weak starting character does not mean an unpleasant starting character. Good Metroidvanias may withhold power, but the basic run/jump/attack loop still needs to feel responsive from the first minute. ([Game Developer][5])

* **Design abilities as verbs, not colored keys.** The best upgrades change how the player moves, fights, reads old rooms, and combines tools. If an “upgrade” is only used once to open one door, it is just a key in disguise. ([Game Developer][5])

* **Block out the game before decorating it.** Hollow Knight used fast black-tile blockouts to test spacing and feel before art passes; Ori used simple paper concepts and polygonal blockouts before set dressing. The shared lesson is: prove traversal, reachability, and room anatomy before spending art time. ([PC Gamer][1])

* **Think in world logic, not just progression logic.** Team Cherry emphasized Hallownest as a coherent place with many meaningful connections, not a genre template. This is why shortcuts, vertical relationships, lakes above rainy cities, and cross-area links matter: they help the player build a mental map. ([PC Gamer][1])

* **Let backtracking recontextualize, not repeat.** Return trips should be faster, stranger, more empowered, or newly dangerous. Dreamnoid’s advice is to make new abilities change how players move through familiar spaces, and Team Cherry explicitly tried to ensure that even being lost could lead to secrets, odd enemies, or unique sights. ([Dreamnoid][6])

* **Plan a critical path, then soften it.** Hollow Knight began with a rough power-up order and world path, then chipped away at hard gating until many routes and skips became possible. The broad pattern is: design the spine first, then add optional branches, shortcuts, sequence breaks, and alternate routes. ([PC Gamer][1])

* **Calibrate “lostness.”** Getting lost can be the soul of the genre, but it needs texture: landmarks, distinctive rooms, save/warp cadence, readable barriers, and enough local rewards that wandering is not empty. The Bandai roundtable’s “wandering speed” and save-point discussion is especially useful here. ([Bandai Namco Entertainment][9])

* **Use rewards to build investment, not just completion percentage.** Good rewards are abilities, shortcuts, lore, weird encounters, health/magic expansions, map knowledge, or currencies that visibly move the player toward a meaningful upgrade. Dreamnoid specifically recommends tying collectibles to abilities or intermediate goals rather than meaningless counters. ([Dreamnoid][6])

* **Avoid over-systematizing discovery into a checklist.** Kayin’s critique is worth taking seriously: if every obstacle is color-coded and every missed item is marked, you may remove friction but also remove mystery. The question should be “what experience is this mechanic serving?” not “what do Metroidvanias usually include?” ([Kayinworks][11])

* **If you use procedural generation, constrain it hard.** Dead Cells’ design writeup says full procedural generation made the game feel illogical and chaotic, so the team moved toward a hybrid approach where algorithms operated within a restrained structure. For Metroidvanias, procedural generation should support pacing and recombination, not replace authored world design. ([Game Developer][10])
